Reading Academic Texts

Main Idea

is the concept that is being discussed or analyzed throughout the entire essay

  • Finding the idea:
    • Have the topic
    • Be a complete sentence
    • Be a sentence that summarizes the details of the paragraph
  • Can be:
    • Thesis Statement
    • Topic Sentence

Thesis Statement

presents the point in a text, in academia its usually presented in an abstract or executive summary found at the last part of introduction

Strategies

  1. read the title and make a guess (infering) on its purpose
  2. Read the first few paragraphs as the thesis statement is located there, if no abstract/exec. summary
  3. Check conclusion as the author sums up and review their main points

Thesis statement can also be identified via Purpose Statement

Cannot be a question

Purpose Statement

introduced by signal phrases that announce the purpose, scope, or direction of the text as well as its focus such as:

  • This study examined...
  • This paper examines...
  • The aim of this essay is to...
  • This paper begins with...
  • The primary objective of this paper is to...
  • The purpose of this essay is to...

Topic Sentence

presents or describes the point of the paragraph. Can be in anywhere in the paragraph

Strategies

  1. Carefully read the 1st sentence
  2. Browse sentences to identify what they mean
  3. Identify the purpose in a paragraph

Outlining

skeleton/backbone of an essay, used in organizing your thoughts before writing it down, making sure to highlight the key ideas in the paragraph or a speech

Types

Formal Outline

follows a format, strict writing

  • Topic Outline
    • 2 types:
      • Alphanumeric Outline
        • Title
        • Thesis Statement:___
        • Introduction
        • I. Body Paragraph 1
          • A. First Point
            • 1. Sub Point
              • a. Subpoint ot Subpoint 1
      • Decimal Outline
        • Title
        • TS:___
        • Introduction
        • I. First body paragraph
          • 1.1 First Point
            • 1.1.1 Subpoint for 1st Point
            • 1.1.2 Subpoint for 1st point
          • 1.2 Second Point
  • Sentence Outline
    • Shows exactly the sentence
    • 2 types:
      • Alphanumeric Outline
      • Decimal Outline
Informal Outline

Drafts or scratch work, requires revision and does not require a format

Example

Subject: children are EXPENSIVE

introduction

  • yeah!
  • maintain for 20 years
  • baby items are more expensive
  • unremovable

body

  • bla bla bla

conclusion

  • babies SUCK

Summarizing, Paraphrasing & Direct Quoting

Summarizing

  • Definition — presents key ideas of the text in your own words, 30% from the original content
  • Guidelines
    1. Clarify your purpose b4 reading
    2. Read and understand the text
    3. Highlight the key words
    4. List ur ideas
    5. Sentence -> Paragraph
    6. No copying sentences from original
    7. Refrain adding comments
    8. Eliminate redundant ideas
    9. Compare original to summary for accuracy
    10. Cite original source
    11. Format summary properly
  • Formats:
    1. Idea Heading
    2. Author Heading
    3. Date Heading

Paraphrasing

  • read and understand text
  • highlight key ideas
  • refrain comments
  • cite original source
  • format
  • 4 rs of paraphrasing
    1. Reword
    2. Rearrange
    3. Realize — some words or phrases cant be changed
    4. Recheck

Direct Quote

  • When you want to respond to verbatim text
  • Guidelines:
    1. copy exact text
    2. use quotation marks
    3. cite original source
    4. format your quotation properly, if <u>></u>40, should be indented

Writing a Reaction Paper

a specialized form of writing where a reader evaluates a scholarly work, work of art, designs, & graphic design

Critical Approaches

  • Formalism
    • posits the key to understanding a text is text itself
    • Questions
      1. does the POV have a particular use?
      2. can the different parts of the work identified and related?
      3. can the different symbols, themes, and motifs identified?
  • Feminism
    • woman focused
  • Reader's Response
    • concerned with the reader's reaction as the work of an audience
  • Marxism
    • economic classes and capitalist system
