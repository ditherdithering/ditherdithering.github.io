Crafting a Research Title

  • Attention Grabbing — A good title captures the reader's interest
  • Clarity Provided — Succinctly conveys the main topic and scope of the study
  • Expectations Set — indicates the research approach, population, and key variables
  • Enhances Searchability — well-chosen keywords make the study easily discoverable in databases

  3. Shapes hypotheses

In a nutshell

  • Conceptual framework is like a roadmap for your research
  • Helps with where you're going (your research questions and objectives), what you need to get there (relevant concepts and variables), and how everything connects (relationships between variables)
  • Keeps your research organized, ensures that you're building on existing knowledge, and makes your study clear and understandable to others
  • Essential for designing your study, collecting and analyzing data, and interpreting your findings within a broader theoretical context

Quantitative Research

  • collecting numerical data via mathematically based methods
  • uses numbers and statistical analysis
    • Aliaga and Gunderson (2000)
  • e.g: decrease of falling numbers in math
Characteristics
  • reliable & objective
  • uses statistics to generalize findings
  • decomplexifies and restructures a problem to limited variables
  • looks at connections between variables and establishes cause & effect (C&E) relationships in highly controlled circumstances
  • tests theories & hypotheses
  • assumes samples are representing the population
  • subjectivity in methodology is secondary concern
  • deals with the details of the subject
Advantages
  • allows to measure and analyze problems and output an objective solution
  • results are reliable as it uses big samples in a population
  • established standards used in choosing instruments, sampling procedures, & choosing the most appropriate statistical treatment
  • personal biases avoided as personal input is not part of the research process
  • simplified processes since the quantitative research are made easy and systematic
  • results are reduced through statistical treatments and can be interpreted in a few attempts
Disadvantages
  • does not consider the natural setting
  • larger study requires researchers to spend more resources
  • results are limited as the data is based on number analysis and not detailed narratives
  • provides less elaborate accounts of human perceptions
  • in experimental research, level of control is normally unapplicable in the real world because it's usually done in a lab
  • preset or fixed alternative answers may not necessarily reflect the participant's true answers
  • findings can be manipulated by researchers' perspective as the participants are unknown to the researchers most of the time
Strengths
  • can be replicated in other contexts
  • provides generalized findings to a large population
  • establishes causality more conclusively
  • makes predictions based on quantifiable data
  • digital data analysis is faster
  • data-gathering techniques are less demanding than qualitative research
  • low degree of subjectivity
  • validity and reliability can be measured
Weaknesses
  • cannot adequately provide in-depth info
  • cannot comprehensively textually describe human experiences
  • numerical data may be insufficient in analyzing intangible factors (gender roles, socio-economic status)
  • less flexible in terms of study design
  • limited responses to what has been asked
Types
  1. Descriptive
    • "Assessing nurses' attitudes towards death and caring for dying students in a comprehensive cancer center"
  2. Correlational
    • "Relationship between service quality and customer satisfaction in the telecommunications industry: evidence from Nigeria"
  3. Ex Post Facto
    • Comparison of personal, social, and academic variables related to university drop-out rate and persistence
  4. Experimental
    • Effects of mobile technology in walking
  5. Quasi-Experimental
    • Impact of smoking bans on smoking
