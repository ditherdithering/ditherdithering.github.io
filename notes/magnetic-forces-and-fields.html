Magnetic Field

A region of space with the ability to exert a magnetic force on dipoles and moving electric charges (\( e^- \)).

Exists around permanent magnets, current-carrying conductors, and other magnetic materials.

Magnetic Force on Moving Charges

Equations

\[ F = qv \cdot B \] where:

  • \( F = \text{the force on a charged particle}, \neq 0 \)
  • \( q = \text{electric charge} \)
  • \( v = \text{velocity of the electric charge, must have a value} \)
  • \( B = \text{magnetic field} \)
  • \( v \cdot B \neq 0 \)

\[ F = q \cdot vB \sin \theta, \hspace{0.3em} B = \frac{F}{qv \sin \theta} \]

  • \( \theta = \text{smaller angle between} \hspace{0.3em} v \hspace{0.3em} \text{and} \hspace{0.3em} B \)

The SI unit of \( B \) is: \[ 1 \, T \, (\text{tesla}) = 1 \, \text{N}/(\text{C} \, \text{m/s}) = 1 \, \text{N}/\text{A} \, \text{m} \]

Alternatively: \[ 1 \, G \, (\text{gauss}) = 1 \cdot 10^{-4} \, T \]

Description

The direction of the magnetic field is the direction of the north pole.

The direction of the cross product can be determined using the right-hand rule.

Magnetic Force on a Current-Carrying Wire

\[ \Sigma q_v = nV, \hspace{1.25em} \text{total number of moving charges} \]

\[ V = AL, \hspace{0.3em} \therefore \Sigma q_v = nAL \]

\[ F = nALqv \, B \, \sin \theta \]

Where:

  • \( nALq = q \)
  • \( v = \frac{L}{t}, \hspace{0.3em} \therefore \)

\[ F = \frac{q}{t}LB \sin \theta = F = ILB \sin \theta \]

A current-carrying wire can also produce its magnetic field, which can be determined with:

  • Biot-Savart's Law -> AC
  • Ampere's Law -> DC

Ampere's Law

Coined by André-Marie Ampère: \[ \int B \cdot dl = \mu_0 I \]

\[ \sum B_i \cdot L_i = \sum B_iL_i \cos \theta_i = \mu_0 I \]

Where:

  • \( \sum B_i \cdot L_i \): summation of the magnetic field times the length \( i \) times
  • \( \sum B_iL_i \cos \theta_i \): summation of the magnetic field times the length with a cosine angle \( i \) times
  • \( \mu_0I \): the equation where \( \mu_0 \) (or \( \epsilon_0 \) in some calculators) is the permeability of a vacuum

\( \mu_0 = 4\pi \cdot 10^{-7} \, \text{Tm/A} \)

  • Relates to currents and magnetic fields they generate
  • Its counterpart is Gauss' Law
  • Uses an arbitrary surface and the closed loop that forms its boundary, called the Amperian Loop

Applications

Magnetic Field on a Long Straight Wire Carrying a Current

\[ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{L} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2 \pi r} \]

Note that the length turns into the circumference of the circle as the magnetic field creates a circular field, due to the wire being straight.

Magnetic Force Between Two Long Parallel Wires

Main equations: Magnetic Force on a Current-Carrying Wire & Magnetic Field on a Long Straight Wire Carrying a Current

\[ F = ILB \sin \theta \] \[ B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2 \pi r} \]

The force of two parallel wires depends on these two factors:

  • They attract if they're parallel, repel if not.

Magnetic Field in a Loop of Wire

\[ B = \frac{\mu_0 NI}{2R} \]

Where \( N \): number of turns from the loop/solenoid/toroid.

Magnetic Field in a Solenoid

Solenoid: a long coil of wire. If tightly wrapped, the magnetic field in its interior is almost uniform.

\[ B = \frac{\mu_0 NI}{L} \]

Magnetic Field in a Toroid

Toroid: a long coil of wire bent into the shape of a torus (donut).

\[ B = \frac{\mu_0 NI}{2 \pi r} \]
