First Aid

Initial assistance to give to an injured person or a suddenly ill person before an ambulance, doctor, or qualified personnel.

Generally performed by the layperson.

Aims of First Aid / 4 P's

  • Preserve: life
  • Prevent: casualties from becoming worse
  • Promote: recovery
  • Protect: the unconscious

Priorities during First Aid

Danger

Assess with:

  • Any dangers to yourself, injured people, or bystanders?

Response

Assess the person for response

Checking for responsiveness

  • Shake shoulder gently
  • Ask if they are alright
  • Should they respond, find out what's wrong and reassess regularly

Levels of Responsiveness

  • Alert
    • Will talk but usually delirious
  • Response to voice
    • Responses to simple commands or open eyes, may respond to simple questions
  • Responds to pain
    • Reacts/makes a noses to a pinch on the back of hand
  • Unresponsive
    • Ditto

Airway

Check and open the airway, place one hand on the forward, tilt the head back and lift the chin

Common Causes of Airway Obstruction

  1. Flaccid Tongue
  2. Foreign Body
  3. Artificial Tooth/Teeth

Breathing

  • Look, listen, and feel for breathing.
  • Look for chest movement and listen for sounds of breathing
    • Do this for no more than 10 seconds.
  • If the person breathes normally, assess for life threatening injuries

Circulation

Look for blood coming out of a wound and control it with blood pressure

Common Injuries for Outdoor Activity

  • Bruise
  • Cut
  • Abrasion - Most Painful wound
  • Sprain
  • Strain

Common First Aid Equipment

Bandage and Bandaging

  • piece of cloth or other material used to bind or wrap a diseased or injured part of the body

Uses of Bandage

  • apply pressure to bleeding wounds
  • cover wounds and burns
  • provides support and immobilization of broken bodes, strains and sprains
  • secures a splint to an injured part of the body

Types of Bandage

  • Gauze
    • Supports dressing material
  • Triangular
    • Padding, support pressure, sling
    • Parts:
      • Apex (points)
      • Ends (tails)
      • Base
  • Elastic
    • Provide support in strains and sprains
  • Tubular
    • Joints support, dressings in place

Ankle Pull

  1. Grasp the victim by both ankles or pant cuffs
  2. Pull with your legs, not your back
  3. Keep your back as straight as possible
  4. Try to keep the pull as straight and in-line as possible
  5. Be aware of the head's unsupported structure

Shoulder Pull

  1. Grasp the victim by the clothing under the shoulders
