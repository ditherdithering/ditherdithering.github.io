Five Literary Themes

Identity

Freedom to draw from cultures and question life

History/Memory

Multiplications of truth and acknowledgement of filtered history due to perspective and experience

Technology

Assistance or Deviance with or against Technology

Intertextuality

Recognize one work as part of the broader history.

Parallels or references are intertextuality.

Electronic Literature

Specifically defined as literature created in the digital medium.

Also known as "born-digital".

Forms of 21st Century Literature

21st Century Literature usually grapples with events, movements, and literature of the past 21st century.

Hypertext/Hyperpoetry

Also known as interactive fiction.

  • Utilizes links as hypertext mark-ups.
  • Can be adventure games, usually visualized as explorative literature.

Animated Poetry

  • Takes a well-written work and gives it a visual representation.
  • The visual aspect is there for the readers/viewers to be immersed by the poem and brings out emotion the writer is trying to portray.

Textula

  • Form of poem that consists of two stanzas with four lines with seven syllables each with a same rhyme at the end of each line.

Blog

Shortened term of weblog, coined in 1997 by a programmer and blogger Jorn Barger.

  • An online platform where one or multiple people can contribute content related to a particular topic.

Chick lit

  • Comprised of books mainly written for women by women.
  • Addresses issues of modern womanhood, often humourously and lightheartedly.

Flash Fiction

Also known as Microfiction.

  • Are complete stories with a small word count.
  • Most Flash Fiction stories range from five words to 1,000.
    • The top end is usually 1,500 words.

Creative Nonfiction

  • Utilizes elements of creative writing to present a factual or true story.
  • Techniques usually reserved for writing fiction can be utilized in Creative Nonfiction.

Digi-Fiction

Also known as Digital Fiction.

  • Combines three medias:
    • Books
    • Movies/Videos
    • Internet Websites

Transmedia Storytelling

  • Integral elements of a fiction get dispersed systematically across multiple delivery channels to create a globalized and coordinated entertainment experience.

Graphic Novels

  • Tells a complete novel via illustrations.
  • Usually longer than Comic Books due to a more definitive story in comparison.

Manga

  • Same as [[#Graphic Novels]] but is more specific to the ones produced in Japan.

Web Novels

  • Novels published online in the internet.
  • Either free to read or paid.
  • Usually serialized, meaning the author can publish stories in installments.
    • i.e: One chapter at a time and/or in a schedule.

Webtoons

  • Digital comics that originated in South Korea and read vertically and by scrolling down on a device.
  • Easily Accessible and free, but each episode takes less than five minutes to read.

Twitterature

Portmanteau of Twitter and literature. (Xiterature?)

  • Uses the microblogging service of Twitter.
  • Includes various genres, such as:
    • Aphorisms
    • Poetry
    • Fiction
    • Any combination thereof
  • Usually written by individuals or in collaboration.
  • With the 280 character limit, it provides writers a creative challenge.

Illustrated Novels

  • Stories through text and illustrated images, with 50% of the narratives are presented visually only.
  • Readers are required to interpret the images to understand the story completely, and textual portions are presented in traditional form. Some illustrated novels may contain no text at all.

Doodle Fiction

  • Literary presentations where an author incorporates doodle drawings and handwritten graphics in place of traditional font.

Speculative Fiction

  • A literary "super genre"
  • Encompasses various genres of fiction with a speculative element
