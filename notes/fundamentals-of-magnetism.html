Magnetism

  • Ability for magnets to attract other magnetic materials
    • They are also called magnets
    • May be natural or artificial
    • Common example: magnetite, lodestone

Magnetic Materials

  • Those attracted by magnets
  • Examples: iron, nickel, cobalt, metal alloys
  • Some artificial magnets can be made using magnetic materials

Non-magnetic Materials

  • Examples: wood, paper, glass, plastic
  • Cannot be attracted by magnets or made using magnetic materials

William Sturgeon

  • Produced the first electromagnet (EM) in 1825

Poles

  • Portions of a magnet found at ends where the magnetic force is the greatest

Magnetization

  • Process of making materials temporarily or permanently magnetic
  • Can be achieved with:
    • Stroking it with a permanent magnet in one direction
    • Allowing an electric current to pass through
    • Induction from Earth's magnetism
      • The magnetic material is brought near a strong magnet's pole in induction
  • Magnetic poles induce a like pole at the far end of the material and an unlike pole in the end adjacent to it
  • The material loses magnetism once the strong magnet is removed
  • Magnetites magnetize due to being exposed to Earth's magnetic field for a long time
  • EMs are human-made and are created using electric current

Magnetic Field (MF)

  • Surrounds a magnet
  • A region of space where a magnet exerts force on a magnetic material
  • Analogous to the electric field (EF) around an electric charge (EC)
  • MFs can be mapped using paper, bar magnets, and magnetic filings/dust

More on Magnetism

  • Similar to electricity

Wilhelm Eduard Weber

A German physicist, theorized that each atom of a magnetic material is a magnet in itself. These atoms arrange themselves into groups called magnetic domains. The magnetic properties of a material depend on how these domains are arranged.

Hans Christian Oersted

  • Danish physicist
  • Shown that a moving charge produces a magnetic field
    • The electron is a charged particle that spins about its axis as it revolves around the nucleus
  • Moving and spinning electrons are sources of magnetism according to the modern theory of magnetism
    • The electron is the smallest possible magnet
    • If an atom has an equal number of electrons spinning in opposite directions, the magnetic fields cancel out and the atom is unmagnetized
    • If there are more electrons spinning in one direction, the atom is magnetized

Types of Magnetic Materials

Ferromagnetic Materials

  • Strongly attracted by a magnet
  • Examples: iron, cobalt, nickel
  • Contain unpaired electrons, giving magnetic properties even without an external magnetic field
  • In a magnetic field, magnetic domains align, producing a strong magnetic field
  • Lose magnetic properties when heated to high temperatures

Paramagnetic Materials

  • Have net magnetic moments due to unpaired electrons
  • Weakly attracted to magnets
  • Examples: transition metals like palladium, platinum, and actinides
  • Become more magnetic when cooled

Diamagnetic Materials

  • Weakly respond to a magnetic field
  • Magnetization exists only with an external magnetic field
  • Magnetic domains align opposite to the applied magnetic field
  • Repelled by a magnetic field
  • Examples: metals, nonmetals, organic compounds
  • Independent of temperature

Curie Point

The temperature at which a magnetic material loses its magnetism is called the Curie temperature (or Curie point). The Curie temperature for cobalt, iron, and nickel are 1131°C, 770°C, and 358°C, respectively.

Geomagnetism

Earth itself is the biggest magnet. Earth's magnetism is due to the motion of charged particles in its liquid outer core. Earth's rotation produces moving charged particles, creating a magnetic field. Earth's magnetism is also referred to as geomagnetism.
