Coulomb's Law

Main idea -> strength of the force of attraction/repulsion between charges

\[ F = \frac{k|q_1q_2|}{r^2} \quad \text{or} \]

\[ F = k\frac{|q_1q_2|}{r^2}, \text{where} \]

F = electrostatic force in N, can be positive or negative since it’s a vector

\[ k = \text{Coulomb's constant}, \quad k = 9 \cdot 10^9N\cdot \frac{m^2}{C^2} \]

\[ r = \text{distance between the charges in m} \]

\[ q_{1,2} = \text{charges in C} \]

\[ F_{12} = F_{21} \]

Example

Given:

\[ q_1 = -25nC \]

\[ q_2 = 15nC \]

\[ r = 0.02m \]

Superposition Principle

  • Each charge exerts a force on another charge such that there are no other forces
  • The total force is the vector sum

Electric Field

Main idea -> charged object surrounded by an electric field where another charged object will experience an electric force

\[ F_e = E|q| \]

Concepts

  • Space surrounding a charged body is an electric field
  • Any charged particle placed in it experiences an electric force
  • Every charge has an electric field

Electric Lines of Force

Discovered by Michael Faraday

Maps out an electric field

  • Starts from + charged particles and end on - charged particles or continue towards \(\infty\)
  • Neither can intersect nor break
  • The greater the number of lines of force, the stronger the field
  • Represented by E

\[ F = \frac{kq}{r^2} \]

Examples

E = 5N/C downward

q = \(1.6 \cdot 10^{-19}\) C

\[ m_e = 9.11 \cdot 10^{-31} \, \text{kg} \]

acceleration?

force?

\[ F_e = E|q| \]

\[ F_e = (5N/C)(|-1.6 \cdot 10^{-19}|) \]

\[ F_e = 8 \cdot 10^{-19} \]

\[ F = ma \quad \text{(use 2nd law of motion)} \]

\[ a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{8 \cdot 10^{-19}}{9.11 \cdot 10^{-31}} = 8.7815587 \cdot 10^{11} \approx 8.78 \, \text{m/s}^2 \]

Electric Field in Conductor

  • Discovered by Faraday, the electric field is 0 inside a closed conducting surface; excess charge is applied on a conductor's surface
  • Example: Faraday's cage
    • Any closed conducting surface is a Faraday cage

Electric Flux

Flow in Latin, a measure of field lines passing through a surface

Unit = Φ

Dot product of E and A

\[ \Phi = E \cdot A = EA \hspace{0.2em} \text{cos}\theta \]

Where θ is the angle between E and A

Note that Φ is scalar with the unit Nm²/C

Example

A = 1.25m²

E = 5 N/C along the x-axis

Φ = ?

Parallel?

Perpendicular?

(a) If parallel, θ = 90, since \(\cos(90) = 0\), \(\therefore \Phi = 0 \, \text{Nm}^2/\text{C}\)

(b) If perpendicular, θ = 0, \(\therefore\):

\[ \Phi = (5)(1.25) \cos(0) \]

\[ \Phi = 6.25 \, \text{Nm}^2/\text{C} \]

Gauss' Law

Founded by Karl Friedrich Gauss, this law connects flux and electric charge

\[ \Phi_{\text{total}} = EA \cos\theta = \frac{q_{\text{total}}}{\epsilon_0} \]

The surface (angled vector and E) is called a Gaussian surface

Charge Distribution

Gauss' law can be used to compute E due to point charges and continuous charge distributions

It must be uniform & symmetrical

It can be expressed in linear, surface, or charge density

Linear Charge Density λ

\[ \lambda = \frac{q}{l} \, \text{C/m}, \text{where l = length} \]

Surface Charge Density σ

\[ \sigma = \frac{q}{A} \, \text{C/m}^2, \text{where A = area} \]

Volume Charge Density ρ

\[ \rho = \frac{q}{V} \, \text{C/m}^3, \text{where V = volume} \]

Example

r = 50cm = 0.5m

q = 150nC = \(150 \cdot 10^{-9} \, \text{C}\)

Sphere

(a) \(\rho = \frac{q}{V} = \frac{150 \cdot 10^{-9}}{\frac{4}{3}\pi(0.5)^3} = 2.86 \cdot 10^{-7}\)

(b) Since θ = 0:

\[ r_1 = 10 \, \text{cm} \]

\[ E_1 = \frac{q_{\text{total}}}{\epsilon_0A} = \frac{\rho V}{\epsilon_0 4\pi r^2_1} = \frac{\rho \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3_1}{\epsilon_0 4\pi r^2_1} = \frac{\rho r_1}{3\epsilon_0} \]

Since \(q_{\text{total}}\) is unknown at 10cm:

\[ E_1 = \frac{(2.86\cdot 10^{-7})(0.1)}{3(\epsilon_0)} = 1079.02 \, \text{N/C} \]

(c) \(r_2 = 65cm\)

\[ E_2 = \frac{q_{\text{total}}}{\epsilon_0 4\pi r^2_2} \]

\[ E_2 = \frac{150 \cdot 10^{-9}}{\epsilon_0 4\pi(0.65)^2} = 3190.85 \, \text{N/C} \]

(b) In a conductor, charges reside at surface area, if \(r = 0.1m\), \(\therefore q_{\text{total}} = 0\), \(E = 0\)
